In this project, I developed a comprehensive content strategy tailored to specific customer personas for my Chinese tattoo design online shop. Knowing that a "one-size-fits-all" approach would be ineffective for this niche, I redesigned the product categories and developed a standardized product description template. By customizing content to address unique user pain points and establishing a blog to capture search intent, I was able to create a more personalized and effective customer journey.

Step 1: Turning Customer Personas Into Product Categories

After developing my customer personas, the first strategic step was to reorganize the product categories and introduce dedicated category landing pages.

Initial Category Structures

Before customer segmentation, my product structure went through two early iterations:

Version 1:

  • Click & Buy: A single page containing all ready-made designs
  • Custom: A preview-only section for custom designs (which I did not have a clearly defined or optimized request journey yet)

Version 2:

  • One Character
  • Two Character Words
  • Short Idioms and Phrases

...the name itself is pretty literal that you can tell what's in each category.

While this version was clearer, it organized products purely by format and character count - making it descriptive, but not behavior-driven.

Persona-Driven Category Redesign

With validated customer personas, I shifted from organizing products by what they are to why customers buy them. The final structure uses intent-based categories aligned with distinct motivations and purchasing behaviors:

  • Virtues & Values – meaning-driven, identity-focused buyers (Targeting Lucas/Sara)
  • Cultural & Heritage – authenticity-seeking, research-oriented buyers (Targeting James/Emily)
  • Fun & Quirky – playful, expressive, low-friction buyers (Targeting Jake)
  • Food & Flavor – novelty lovers, culture-curious buyers (Targeting Jake/Sara)
  • Minimal & Aesthetic – design-sensitive, visually driven buyers (Targeting Ava)

(Each category aligns with a specific persona defined in the marketing strategy. For detailed persona descriptions, check out: How do I Create my Customer Personas Without CRM )

This evolution - from product-based categorization to intent-based navigation - resulted in a clearer, more intuitive browsing experience tailored to real user decision-making.

Step 2: Designing Product Pages Around User Questions

After creating the product category pages, the next key question was: What content should each product page include?

At this stage, I did not yet have a finalized content structure or page layout. However, I know that every section on the product page must serve a purpose and answer a specific user question. The goal was to ensure that customers could quickly and intuitively find the information they needed to make a confident decision.

User Intent Analysis

I therefore started with user intent - identifying what customers want to know - and mapped each question to a corresponding piece of content:

Customer Questions Content Provided
Q: What does this character mean? A: Detailed explanation of the character's meaning
Q: Is this culturally accurate? A: Cultural context and historical background
Q: How was it created? Is this truly handwritten? A: Handwriting and creation process video
Q: What exactly will I receive after purchase? A: Clear description of file deliverables
Q: What will it look like on skin? A: Tattoo mockup images for visualization
Q: Why do people choose this design? A: Emotional reasoning behind the tattoo choice

UX Design drafting

Once the content framework was defined, I translated it into a simplified UX layout. Because I am also the front end developer for this project, the objective was not pixel-perfect design, but a clear structural blueprint showing what content appears where. Detailed refinements were made iteratively during development.

Outcome

The resulting product page provides a clear, trust-building experience that balances education, emotion, and conversion. While certain sections can be expanded or reduced to better align with specific personas, this structure serves as a scalable foundation for all future product pages.

Step 3: Customizing Product Pages by Personas

Once I established a standardized product page template, I customized each page based on its category - defined by the underlying customer personas. The goal was to address each persona's core pain points by prioritizing the content they value most, while removing or de-emphasizing elements they find less relevant.

Meaning-Driven Category

(Virtues & Values, Cultural & Heritage)

For these users, trust and authenticity are the primary barriers to conversion. Content depth is intentionally more detailed, as these customers prefer to reflect and validate meaning before committing.

What I customized:

  • Handwritten process videos to demonstrate originality
  • In-depth cultural and historical explanations to establish authority
  • Used a calm, respectful, and reflective tone throughout the page

Product page for Cultural & Heritage category

Fun & Quirky Category

For this persona, too much seriousness reduces engagement. Content is optimized for emotional impulse and enjoyment, not deep research.

What I customized:

  • Removed handwriting process videos
  • Used witty, light product descriptions
  • Added playful sections like "Quirk Level: 8/10"
  • Kept pages fast, scannable, and entertaining

Witty and light product descriptions

Minimal & Aesthetic Category

For this persona, visual impact outweighs narrative depth. The focus is on design, mood, and aesthetic appeal.

What I customized:

  • Short, evocative phrases (e.g., "Rooted in ancient pictographs") to enhance visual storytelling
  • Kept handwriting process videos, positioned as a purely aesthetic and atmospheric element

Step 4: Creating a Blog Section to Drive Organic Traffic

To drive organic traffic, I implemented a blog section built around search intent, rather than keyword stuffing. The objective was to attract high-intent users by answering real questions and guiding them naturally toward relevant products.

I won't go into full detail here - for a deeper breakdown of the blog strategy, check out: How do I Implement Keyword-Driven On-Page SEO.

Below is a high-level summary of the approach:

  • Information depth: I created educational, long-form pillar content that addresses deeper cultural and linguistic questions that James Chen (The Cultural Roots Seeker) would ask. Topics are intentionally people-first (e.g., how to choose a meaningful Chinese tattoo, common cultural mistakes, and calligraphy style explained) and written from the perspective of an expert - not a salesperson.
  • Readability: Content is structured with clear section hierarchy and digestible formatting to ensure accessibility for both research-driven readers and visually oriented browsers.
  • Commercial design: Every blog post includes multiple commercial entry points, which are product links embedded as tattoo inspiration images, connecting educational content directly to relevant product pages and converting readers into customers. Most posts ends with a context-appropriate CTA, aligned with the user's stage in the decision journey.

I continuously refined this people-first content strategy, so it'll better support top-of-funnel discovery, authority building, and long-term organic traffic grow.

Summary

By optimizing content around customer intent, I shifted from creating content for traffic to creating content for relevance. This ensures that every article speaks directly to what users are actually trying to achieve - improving engagement, guiding decision-making, and increasing conversion efficiency across the funnel.

My key takeaways in this project:

  • Focus on user intent, and make browsing intuitive and engaging
  • Answer user questions, build product pages around personas' needs
  • Design content to both educate and lead naturally to action
