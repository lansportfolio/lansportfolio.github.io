How I Made My Website Crawlable for Search Engines

After building a website that's functioning for product browsing, directing to checkout page and completing purchasing, I decided to get my site indexed by search engines, so that I could get more customer data and optimize my site with active users real data.

In this project summary, I will detail how I managed the entire lifecycle of how a search engine bot discovers, parses, and saves my site through proactive discovery strategy.

Step 1: Establishing the Technical "Handshake"

Search engines don't just "find" websites; they need to be invited. The first step is creating the infrastructure that makes discovery possible.

  • Verified Ownership via Webmaster Tools: I registered and verified the site with Google Search Console (GSC) and Bing Webmaster Tools. This creates a direct communication line between the site and the search engine, allowing for manual indexing requests and error reporting.
  • XML Sitemap Deployment: I generated a dynamic XML sitemap‚Äîa digital "table of contents"‚Äîand submitted it directly to GSC. This ensures that the crawler doesn't have to "guess" the site structure and can find all high-priority pages immediately.

Step 2: Removing "Crawl Friction"

A new site is often fragile in the eyes of a bot. I performed a "Pre-Index Audit" to ensure there were no technical barriers.

  • Robots.txt Configuration: I audited the robots.txt file to ensure that essential directories were "Allowed" and that no "Disallow" strings were accidentally blocking the main content.
  • Index Tag Verification: I used a crawler to verify that no noindex meta tags were left over from the development/staging phase, which is a common mistake that prevents new sites from appearing in search results.
  • Canonical Integrity: I implemented self-referencing canonical tags on all pages to signal to Google that these are the "master" versions of the content, preventing duplicate content issues before they start.

Step 3: Implementing "Push" vs. "Pull" Indexing

Traditionally, you wait for a bot to "pull" data (crawl). In 2026, professional SEOs "push" data for near-instant results.

  • Manual URL Inspection: For high-value landing pages (like the Tattoo Shop homepage), I used the URL Inspection Tool in GSC to "Request Indexing," moving those pages to the top of Google‚Äôs priority queue.
  • IndexNow Protocol: I implemented the IndexNow API, which instantly notifies search engines (like Bing and Yandex) every time a new design or blog post is published, updated, or deleted. This reduces indexing latency from days to seconds.

Step 4: Signaling Authority through Discovery Links

Search engine bots are "spiders" that travel across the web via links. To speed up discovery, I created "External Signals."

  • Internal Linking Architecture: I ensured that the homepage linked directly to major category pages, creating a "flat" architecture that allows bots to reach any page within three clicks.
  • Social & External Pings: By sharing the new site on high-authority platforms (LinkedIn, Pinterest, and niche design forums), I created "referral traffic" and external links. When a bot crawls those high-authority sites and finds a link to my new site, it follows it and begins the indexing process naturally.

Step 5: Monitoring the Indexation Lifecycle

The job isn't done until the data shows up in the "Pages" report.

  • Validation: I monitored the Indexing Report in GSC to track the status of "Crawled - currently not indexed" vs. "Indexed."
  • Rich Results Testing: I used the Rich Results Test tool to ensure that my Schema Markup was being parsed correctly, ensuring that when the site is indexed, it appears with enhanced features like price, ratings, and FAQ snippets.

The first step is to let Google know your site exists.

  • Enter your full site URL (e.g., https://yoursite.github.io/).

This step essentially registers your website with Google, opening the door for indexing and search visibility.

2. Verify Ownership

Next, Google needs to confirm that you actually own the site before indexing it. There are several verification methods:

  • HTML file: Upload a verification file to your Hexo site.
  • HTML tag: Add a verification meta tag to your site's <head> section.
  • Google Analytics: Use an existing GA property to verify ownership.

3. Submit Your Sitemap

A sitemap gives Google a clear structure of your website, making the indexing process faster and more efficient.

Although submitting a sitemap is optional for websites with fewer than 500 pages, it's still a best practice.

  • Generate a sitemap for your Hexo site.
  • Go to Sitemaps in GSC and enter your sitemap URL (e.g., https://yoursite.com/sitemap.xml).
